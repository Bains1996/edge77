The $26 billion problem

Carrier billing errors cost the freight industry an estimated $26 billion annually. That's not a typo. Across LTL, FTL, parcel, and intermodal shipping, carriers routinely overcharge shippers — and most of it goes undetected.

Why does this happen? Modern freight billing is extraordinarily complex. A single invoice may contain dozens of line items: base rates, fuel surcharges, residential delivery fees, liftgate charges, appointment fees, reconsignment fees, and more. Multiply that across thousands of invoices per month, and errors become inevitable.

The problem isn't always malice. Many overcharges stem from system errors, tariff misapplication, or outdated rate tables. But regardless of the cause, the financial impact on shippers is real and significant.

Most common overcharge types

Fuel Surcharge Errors

Carriers apply fuel surcharges based on weekly DOE diesel indices. Common errors include using the wrong index week, applying the surcharge percentage to the base rate instead of total freight charges, or failing to honor fuel caps negotiated in your contract.

Weight Discrepancies

Carriers often reclassify shipments to higher weight brackets after pickup. This is especially common in LTL freight, where dimensional weight calculations can dramatically change the billed amount. A 500-lb shipment reclassified to 800 lbs can cost hundreds of extra dollars.

Accessorial Fee Abuse

Accessorials are the most opaque part of freight billing. Carriers charge extra for liftgate service, residential delivery, inside delivery, limited access, and dozens of other situations. Many of these charges are applied without proper justification or at inflated rates.

Duplicate Charges

System errors sometimes result in the same charge appearing multiple times on a single invoice. Without automated detection, these duplicates can easily slip through manual review.

Incorrect Distance or Zone Calculation

For zone-based rating, carriers may calculate distances using outdated maps or apply the wrong zone to a lane. These errors compound across high-volume lanes.

How to identify overcharges manually vs. with AI

Manual identification requires a dedicated team comparing invoice line items against your rate contracts, one invoice at a time. A skilled analyst can review 30-50 invoices per day — but most shippers only audit 10-20% of invoices due to resource constraints.

The problem is clear: if you audit only 15% of invoices, you're missing errors in 85% of your freight spend. And even within the invoices you audit, human fatigue leads to missed errors after hours of review.

AI-powered identification flips the equation. Automated systems ingest 100% of invoices, extract every field, and cross-reference each charge against your negotiated rates. They never get tired, never miss a line item, and process thousands of invoices in the time it takes a human to review a handful.

The difference in detection rates is staggering. Shippers using automated audit typically discover 3-5x more overcharges than those relying on manual review alone.

The dispute process: Step by step

Once you've identified an overcharge, here's how the recovery process works:

Step 1: Document the Error

Gather the invoice, your rate contract, and evidence of the discrepancy. This includes the specific line item, the contracted rate, and the overcharged amount. Clear documentation is critical for a successful dispute.

Step 2: File a Formal Dispute

Submit a dispute to the carrier's claims department. Most carriers have specific portals or email addresses for billing disputes. Include your account number, invoice number, tracking number, and a clear explanation of the error.

Step 3: Follow Up Relentlessly

Carriers typically take 30-90 days to resolve disputes. Many shippers give up after the first follow-up. Persistent follow-up — often multiple contacts over weeks — is essential to actually recover your money.

Step 4: Receive Credit Memo or Refund

Successful disputes result in a credit memo applied to future invoices or a direct refund. Track these credits to ensure they're actually applied to your account.

Why contingency-based audit is the lowest-risk approach

Traditional freight audit companies charge monthly platform fees regardless of whether they find errors. This model shifts the risk to you — you pay whether they save you money or not.

Contingency-based pricing eliminates this risk entirely. You only pay a percentage of the overcharges that are actually recovered. If the audit finds nothing, you pay nothing. Zero upfront cost, zero ongoing fees, zero risk.

This model also aligns incentives. The audit provider is motivated to find every possible overcharge because their revenue depends on it. With fixed-fee models, there's no financial incentive to dig deeper or run additional validation passes.
